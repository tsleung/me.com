bayes

what one observation actually tells you

three demos about inference from almost nothing: a test result, a glance at a wall, a serial number. in each one the honest answer is unintuitive — and computable on one hand.

01 · base rates

the test says you're guilty

an anti-cheat system catches 90% of cheaters. it wrongly flags an honest player only 10% of the time. one player in ten cheats. you get flagged.

how likely is it you're actually cheating? most people say 90%. the answer is 50% — a coin flip.

the trap: the test runs on everyone, and honest players outnumber cheaters nine to one. 90 cheaters get flagged — and so do 90 innocents. "90% accurate" describes the test, not the verdict.

this isn't a toy failure. given the same structure for breast-cancer screening (1% prevalence, 79% sensitivity, 9.6% false-positive rate), 95 of 100 physicians put the answer at 70–80%. it's about 8% (eddy 1982). in a 2007 study only 21% of gynecologists got it right — until the same numbers were rephrased as counts of people instead of probabilities, after which 87% did (gigerenzer et al. 2007). the demo above counts people for exactly that reason.

02 · the copernican principle

how long will the wall stand?

in 1969 the astrophysicist j. richard gott stood at the berlin wall — then 8 years old — and asked what he could predict from its age alone.

if the moment you look is nothing special, you are equally likely to be anywhere in a thing's lifetime. even odds you're somewhere in the middle half. from that alone: 50% confidence the wall would stand between 2⅔ and 24 more years (as he later told it — the story reached print in the new yorker in 1999). it fell 20 years later.

the median forecast is always the same move: double what you've seen. gott published the method in nature in 1993 and scored it against the wall, the ussr he'd visited in 1977 (gone 14 years later), and — the day the paper appeared — all 44 broadway and off-broadway shows then running. by 1999, 36 had closed, every one inside its predicted window.

the fine print (caves 2000): the rule is licensed only when age is genuinely all you know. a 90-year-old is not halfway through a life — actuarial tables beat the copernican prior the moment you have them. and your look must be unspecial: you can't apply it to a marriage while attending the wedding.

03 · the german tank problem

count the tanks

the allies wanted german monthly tank production. conventional intelligence — informants, aerial photos, extrapolated factory capacity — said about 1,400 a month. the statisticians ignored all of it and read the serial numbers on captured tanks.

if serials run 1, 2, 3, … then the largest serial you've seen, plus the average gap between the ones you hold, estimates the whole run: N ≈ m + m/k − 1. their answer: 246 a month. german ministry records, captured after the war: 245.

month by month, the same picture (ruggles & brodie 1947):

german tank production, estimated three ways
month serial-number estimate intelligence estimate german records
june 1940 169 1,000 122
june 1941 244 1,550 271
august 1942 327 1,550 342

with a single captured tank the formula collapses to 2m − 1: double what you see. the same move as the wall — one observation, assumed unspecial, doubled. the method was reused on panther road wheels (february 1944: estimated 270 tanks, records say 276) and, most successfully, on v-2 rockets.

postscript

the same trick everywhere

  • the lindy effect — for non-perishables (technologies, ideas), every year survived is evidence of more to come. named for lindy's deli in new york, 1964; formalized by mandelbrot.
  • laplace's rule of succession — after n sunrises and no failures, p(sunrise tomorrow) = (n+1)/(n+2). the 18th-century version of the same humility.
  • the doomsday argument — apply the wall logic to your own birth rank among all humans ever born. gott's paper does exactly this; uncomfortable reading.
